The Essential Guide to New Zealand’s Wine Regions

New Zealand’s wine regions aren’t just about what’s in the glass — they shape the pace, atmosphere, and architecture of the places you stay. Here’s a guide to the six regions that do it best, and what makes each one worth building a trip around.

Marlborough

Known for: Sauvignon Blanc, wide skies, quiet roads New Zealand’s most famous wine region is also its most open and sun-filled. Think vineyard-lined country drives, coastal detours, and cellar doors where the winemakers might still pour the glass. It’s also one of the easiest regions to stay among the vines, with boutique cottages and vineyard lodges dotted across the Wairau and Awatere Valleys.

Central Otago

Known for: Pinot Noir, alpine scenery, gold-rush towns Dramatic landscapes meet delicate reds in this high-country region. Gibbston, Bannockburn, and Cromwell are full of boutique vineyards and cellar doors, while Queenstown offers high-end comfort just a short drive away. Here, a vineyard stay isn’t just scenic — it often includes mountain views from the bed.

Hawke’s Bay

Known for: Syrah, architecture, long lunch culture Hawke’s Bay leans toward warmth — in both climate and hospitality. The region blends serious wine with relaxed charm. You’ll find colonial inns, vineyard cottages, and modern stays all within a short bike ride of cellar doors. With strong ties to food, it’s also a standout for wine weekends that revolve around shared plates and open patios.

Wairarapa

Known for: Boutique producers, village atmosphere Just over the Remutaka Range from Wellington, the Wairarapa region centres on Martinborough — a small, elegant town wrapped by vines. It’s walkable, slow-paced, and ideal for a low-effort getaway. Many of the cellar doors are in converted homes or barns, and the accommodation often carries the same detail and charm.

Waipara (North Canterbury)

Known for: Pinot Noir, Riesling, under-the-radar stays Waipara is smaller, quieter, and more rugged than the others. Just north of Christchurch, it’s gaining recognition for producers who are pushing boundaries in a grounded, organic way. The stays here reflect that — minimalist but thoughtful, peaceful but close to the vines.

Waiheke Island

Known for: Island vineyards, coastal views, weekend energy More than just wine — Waiheke is about pace. The island’s vineyards spill toward beaches, and tasting rooms are often perched on hillsides with sea views. Stay in a cottage overlooking the vines, or near a tasting room with a view of the Gulf. It’s an easy trip from Auckland, but rarely feels like the city

Waiheke Island Wineries Worth Booking a Night For

Waiheke isn’t short on great wine or great views — but for the full effect, staying overnight makes all the difference. Here are three vineyard-connected wineries on the island where it’s worth checking in for more than just a tasting.

1. Man O’ War

Located on the island’s eastern edge, Man O’ War is one of the most remote and rewarding destinations. The cellar door sits on a beach with views across the Hauraki Gulf, and the on-site cottages let you stay long after the last tasting. Expect rustic charm, open water, and a sky full of stars.

2. Mudbrick Vineyard & Restaurant

Just minutes from the ferry terminal but entirely its own world, Mudbrick combines elegance with location. The on-site accommodation ranges from private cottages to full vineyard houses. With sweeping views back toward Auckland and a reputation for long lunches that turn into dinners, it’s a smart choice for couples or small groups.

3. Cable Bay Vineyards

Cable Bay offers sleek interiors and strong wine — and their guesthouse matches the tone. Perched above the vines with modern architecture and open-plan spaces, it’s perfect for travellers who want something refined but relaxed. Walk to the beach, the cellar door, or stay in and enjoy the view.

Why Stay Overnight?

Most Waiheke visitors take the last ferry back. Staying lets you skip the rush, slow the pace, and actually take in the island’s energy after the crowds leave. With good wine, good views, and no need to drive — it’s the right way to do it.

How the Screwcap Changed New Zealand Wine (and Travel)

In the early 2000s, a group of New Zealand winemakers made a decision that would reshape the industry: they swapped the cork for the screwcap.

What seemed simple at the time turned out to be a bold statement. It said: we back the quality of what’s in the bottle — and we’re not waiting for permission. It cut out pretence, improved reliability, and signalled to the world that New Zealand wine was serious about doing things differently.

So why name a travel site after it?

Because the same idea applies to travel.

Screwcap as a name is about clarity over clutter. It's about skipping the elaborate and focusing on what works — sharp stays, good settings, and a strong sense of what you're getting. Just like the screwcap changed how people trusted a bottle of wine, we think the same can be done for finding the right place to stay.
